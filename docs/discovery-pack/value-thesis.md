# Farmmap — Value Thesis

## The Problem Worth Solving

The Farm Retail Association's own consumer research identifies "don't know where to
find one" as the primary barrier to farm shop purchases among UK adults who already
want to buy local food. The FSA's Food and You 2 survey (Wave 7, 2024) places this
population at approximately 22.3 million UK adults who value local food, of whom
a meaningful fraction — estimated at 3.6 million — have never visited a farm shop
specifically because of the discovery friction. That is a large, identifiable, real
problem with a straightforward solution: a good map.

## The Economic Case

Eliminating that discovery friction would, at base case, route approximately 360,000
consumers into farm shops who are not currently visiting them. At an average first-year
spend of £225 per new customer, that is £137 million in annual consumer spend
unlocked by improved discovery. Even the worst-case scenario — 4% of the gap
converted, lower average spend — produces £43 million. These are not venture-scale
figures in the sense of capturing a large percentage; they are large enough that
capturing a fraction of one percent in subscription and commission revenue builds
a sustainable product.

The unit economics on the supply side are equally compelling. ValueBench estimates
that a farm shop receiving 10 genuinely new regular customers per year from Farmmap
earns approximately £2,000 in incremental gross profit. The Bronze subscription
costs that shop £240 per year. The value delivery ratio at base case is more than
8:1. Even in the worst case, where Farmmap drives only 3 new customers per year per
shop, the gross profit delivered (£600) exceeds the Bronze subscription cost
(£240) by a factor of 2.5. This matters for sales: the subscription is defensible
not as a charity payment to a new directory but as a return-positive marketing
spend.

## The Standalone Constraint

Year 3 base-case revenue for Farmmap alone is approximately £164,000. Year 5 is
approximately £450,000. These figures are real and achievable, but they are
insufficient to support a well-paid founding team or produce a venture-scale outcome.
The 20% Bronze penetration rate of claimed listings assumed in the base case by Year 3
is not conservative — it requires genuine consumer traffic, active sales outreach, and
a credible value demonstration. Getting there demands execution, not just a product.

The standalone constraint is not a flaw in the model; it is the honest output of a
freemium directory business targeting a fragmented market of small operators.
Comparable directory businesses at this scale — local verticals with 200–400 paying
subscribers — are viable lifestyle businesses. They are not venture-scale outcomes.

## The Portfolio Multiplier

The standalone constraint is why the portfolio thesis is the correct investment
frame. Seven verticals sharing one engine — Farmmap, TractorMap, BerthMap,
CampingMap, ForecourtMap, FishMap, BrewMap — each targeting a similarly fragmented,
discovery-deficient market, each built from a configuration file and a seed dataset
once the engine exists, projects to a combined Year 5 base-case revenue of
£2.2 million. At a conservative 5× revenue multiple, that is an approximately
£11 million enterprise value.

The portfolio multiplier is approximately 4.9× the single-vertical Y5 revenue. The
incremental cost of each additional vertical (estimated at £15,000–25,000 for data
acquisition, domain, and design customisation) is far below the incremental revenue
each generates. The shared engine means infrastructure costs do not scale with
vertical count. The shared consumer audience — a user of CampingMap is a plausible
user of FarmMap and BrewMap — creates cross-directory discovery effects that further
improve the unit economics of later verticals.

## The Load-Bearing Assumption

The entire commercial thesis depends on a single assumption: the free directory
reaches and sustains approximately 100,000 monthly visitors. Without that consumer
traffic, there is no credible Bronze pitch, no Silver GMV, no Gold marketing service
value. Every revenue line in the model traces back to the free tier working.

This is not a weakness in the thesis — it is a clarity. Farmmap has one primary job
in years one and two: attract and retain consumers. If it does that, the commercial
model follows. If it does not, the commercial model does not recover. The SEO
strategy, content programme, and link-building plan are therefore not marketing
add-ons; they are the business.

## Summary

Farmmap's value thesis is sound, specific, and grounded in credible sector data.
The problem is real. The unit economics work. The standalone revenue is modest but
the portfolio return is compelling. The investment case is strongest when framed
as an engine business with Farmmap as the proof-of-concept vertical — not as a
single directory with modest upside.
