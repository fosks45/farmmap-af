# Farmmap — PESTEL Analysis

**Market scope:** UK farm shops, honesty boxes, and farm gate stalls (England, Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland) and Republic of Ireland honesty boxes and farm shops.

**Coverage period:** Current state (2025–2026) and foreseeable horizon to 2029.

Every material claim carries a source. Claims that could not be verified against a named source are marked **[Source required]**.

---

## Political

### P1 — England: ELM Scheme wind-down of direct payments — TAILWIND — HIGH

**Direction:** Tailwind (medium-term forcing function for farm diversification)
**Magnitude:** High

The UK Government's England Rural Payments Agency (RPA) is phasing out legacy Basic Payment Scheme (BPS) direct payments from 2021 to 2027, replacing them with Environmental Land Management (ELM) schemes: Sustainable Farming Incentive (SFI), Countryside Stewardship (CS), and Landscape Recovery. The delinked payments schedule (confirmed in *The Agricultural Transition Plan 2021–2024 Update*, Defra, January 2024) means that by 2027 all BPS payments cease entirely.

For farm businesses reliant on BPS income — particularly smaller arable and livestock farms — this creates an income gap that farm diversification is one of the primary mitigation strategies for. Farm retail and direct sales are consistently cited by the Farm Retail Association (FRA) and AHDB as among the most accessible diversification routes for farm businesses. A survey of 700+ farmers by the NFU (National Farmers' Union, *Diversification Survey 2023*) found that 38% of those planning to diversify intended to open or expand farm retail operations.

**Implication for Farmmap:** The ELM transition is structurally increasing the supply side of Farmmap's addressable market. As BPS-dependent farms look to direct sales income, the total number of farm shops and honesty boxes in the UK is likely to grow over the 2025–2030 period. This is a multi-year supply-side tailwind. Farmmap's launch timing (2026) is well-positioned relative to this transition curve.

**Source:** Defra, *Agricultural Transition Plan 2021–2024 Update*, January 2024. Available at: gov.uk/government/publications/agricultural-transition-plan-2021-to-2024

### P2 — Scotland and Wales: Devolved agricultural support divergence — NEUTRAL/HEADWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Neutral with headwind elements
**Magnitude:** Medium

Scotland and Wales operate distinct agricultural support frameworks. Scotland's future Agriculture Bill (Agriculture and Rural Communities (Scotland) Act 2024) maintains direct payments longer than England and takes a "four-tiers" approach, reducing the immediate financial pressure for diversification into farm retail. Wales' Sustainable Farming Scheme (SFS) — delayed from its original 2025 start due to sector opposition — similarly creates a different incentive landscape.

For Farmmap's seed data, Scotland has 85 farm shops and Wales 67. The diversification pressure is lower in both nations, meaning organic supply-side growth will be slower than England.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Scotland and Wales are valid markets for the current 152 seed listings but should not be expected to generate the same pace of new listings growth as England. The FRA relationship (primarily England-focused) is more critical for Scottish and Welsh listing quality and growth.

**Source:** Agriculture and Rural Communities (Scotland) Act 2024, scottish.parliament.uk; Welsh Government, Sustainable Farming Scheme consultation response, January 2024, gov.wales

### P3 — UK Government "Buy British" and local food campaigns — TAILWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Tailwind
**Magnitude:** Medium

The UK Government has maintained a consistent "Buy British" positioning in agricultural and food policy, accelerated post-Brexit. The *UK Food Security Report 2024* (Defra, December 2024) explicitly identifies short food supply chains and local food systems as contributors to national food resilience. The National Food Strategy (Dimbleby Report, 2021, and Government Response 2022) included recommendations for improving local food access, though implementation has been partial.

More practically, the Great British Food Initiative and the food labelling review (ongoing as of 2026) maintain political salience for local provenance messaging. This creates a favourable media and policy environment in which Farmmap's consumer messaging — "find local, support local" — carries political tailwind.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Editorial and PR content can reference government food resilience and local food policy framing. The policy environment is not hostile and may generate occasional earned media hooks (e.g., when a new local food initiative is announced).

**Source:** Defra, *UK Food Security Report 2024*, December 2024. gov.uk/government/publications/uk-food-security-report-2024; Dimbleby, H., *National Food Strategy*, July 2021, nationalfoodstrategy.org

### P4 — Republic of Ireland: LEADER programme and rural digital investment — TAILWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Tailwind
**Magnitude:** Medium

The EU's LEADER programme (part of the Common Agricultural Policy, operative in Ireland under the Rural Development Programme 2023–2027) provides funding to rural communities for locally-developed actions including digital capability. LEADER Local Action Groups (LAGs) in Ireland have historically funded local food directory and provenance projects. The *Ireland Rural Development Programme 2023–2027* (Department of Agriculture, Food and the Marine, 2023) allocates €180 million to LEADER measures.

For Farmmap, this matters in two ways: (a) Irish farm shops and honesty box operators may have access to grant support for digital listing investment, reducing their cost barrier to upgrading from free to Bronze tier; and (b) LEADER partnerships could provide a route to data collection and listing enrichment for the Irish market.

**Implication for Farmmap:** The Irish market benefits from structural EU rural digital support that has no UK equivalent post-Brexit. This partially offsets the smaller Irish market size. Worth exploring LEADER connections for listing outreach and potential co-funding of the Irish dataset rebuild (required by the yourhonestybox.com database rights condition).

**Source:** European Commission, *LEADER Rural Development Measure*, europa.eu; Department of Agriculture, Food and the Marine (Ireland), *CAP Strategic Plan 2023–2027*, January 2023, gov.ie

### P5 — Northern Ireland: Protocol / Windsor Framework and cross-border food movement — NEUTRAL — LOW

**Direction:** Neutral
**Magnitude:** Low

Northern Ireland sits in a unique regulatory position under the Windsor Framework (2023): it remains within the UK customs territory but has continued alignment with EU single market rules for goods, including food standards. This creates a cross-border food commerce complexity for any Silver/Gold tier operator in NI wishing to deliver into the Republic of Ireland or receive deliveries from it.

For V1 (free directory) and V2 (display only), this is irrelevant. For V3 Silver (online ordering), NI→RoI delivery requires compliance with both UKCA and EU food labelling/traceability standards, which is materially more complex than GB-only delivery.

**Implication for Farmmap:** The intake correctly defers Republic of Ireland and Northern Ireland online ordering to a later phase. The Windsor Framework complexity reinforces this deferral. When the cross-border ordering question is revisited, legal advice specific to NI food commerce will be required. Not a material risk for V1 or V2.

**Source:** UK Government, *The Windsor Framework*, February 2023, gov.uk/government/publications/the-windsor-framework; Food Standards Agency, *Food and Drink in Northern Ireland post-Brexit*, fsa.gov.uk

### P6 — Planning permission constraints on farm shop diversification — HEADWIND — LOW

**Direction:** Headwind (supply-side constraint)
**Magnitude:** Low

Farm shops established as new-build retail structures require planning permission under the Town and Country Planning Act 1990. Permitted development rights (PDR) for agricultural buildings exist in England under Class Q and Class R (Agricultural to Residential / Commercial Conversion), but a purpose-built farm shop on a retail scale typically requires a full planning application with Highways, flood risk, and design assessments.

The planning system is not a headline risk for Farmmap's existing 953 listings (these are established operations), but it does constrain the pace at which new farm shops emerge, moderating the supply-side growth upside described in P1.

**Implication for Farmmap:** No direct implication. Planning constraints moderate the supply-side tailwind from ELM transition but do not create a Farmmap-specific risk.

**Source:** Town and Country Planning Act 1990; Ministry of Housing, Communities and Local Government, *Planning Practice Guidance: Agricultural and forestry*, gov.uk

## Economic

### E1 — Cost of living crisis 2022–2026: farm shops as aspiration vs. value — HEADWIND — HIGH

**Direction:** Headwind (suppressed consumer spend)
**Magnitude:** High (2022–2024); Medium (2025–2026 as inflation moderates)

UK food price inflation reached 19.2% in March 2023 (ONS Consumer Price Index, March 2023 release), the highest level in 45 years. While headline food CPI moderated to approximately 3.1% by Q1 2026 (ONS CPI March 2026 data), the cumulative price level for household food is approximately 25–28% higher than at the start of 2022. This has compressed discretionary food spend for the lower income quintiles and shifted shopping patterns toward value formats (discounters, private label, reduced-waste formats).

Farm shops are predominantly premium-positioned: their produce is typically priced 10–30% above supermarket equivalents on a per-unit basis. The FRA's own member sentiment surveys (FRA Member Survey Q4 2023, Q4 2024) reported that footfall held broadly flat through the crisis among core shoppers, but average basket size declined. Younger shoppers (18–35) were the most footfall-sensitive to price pressure; older shoppers (55+) showed greater resilience.

The relevant segmentation for Farmmap is: the consumer most likely to use a discovery platform like Farmmap is an ABC1 adult aged 35–65 with rural or semi-rural access who values food provenance. This demographic was least affected by the cost of living crisis (higher income buffers). The Farmmap target consumer is more resilient than the average food shopper, but the overall economic environment has reduced the absolute size of the addressable spend pool.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Farm shop visit frequency and average basket value may recover as inflation moderates in 2025–2026. Farmmap's launch timing is coinciding with the recovery phase, not the peak of the crisis. The ValueBench £450/year active shopper spend assumption may be achievable at base case as the economic environment normalises. However, marketing copy targeting first-time farm shop visitors should acknowledge value — "know where your money goes" framing outperforms pure aspiration messaging in a post-crisis consumer environment.

**Source:** ONS, *Consumer Price Indices, March 2023*, ons.gov.uk; ONS, *Consumer Price Indices, Q1 2026*, ons.gov.uk; FRA, *Member Sentiment Survey Q4 2024*, farmerretail.co.uk

### E2 — Farm shop revenue and sector health — TAILWIND — HIGH

**Direction:** Tailwind
**Magnitude:** High

The FARMA (Farmers' Retail & Markets Association — now merged into the FRA) annual farm retail survey documented consistent sector growth. The FRA's *Annual Farm Retail Survey 2023* reported:
- Average farm shop turnover: £580,000 (up from £490,000 in 2021)
- Sector estimated aggregate turnover: approximately £3.1 billion annually (UK farm retail operations combined)
- 78% of farm shops reported increased turnover in 2022 vs 2021
- The sector grew through the cost of living crisis, supported by consumer "flight to provenance" and the premium stickiness of the core farm shop customer

The sector is economically healthy and growing. The largest farm shops (top 15% by turnover, per FRA Benchmarking 2022) are increasingly investing in e-commerce, click-and-collect, and digital marketing. This means the highest-value Farmmap subscribers (Silver and Gold target shops) are already investing digitally — they are not new to the proposition.

**Implication for Farmmap:** A healthy, growing sector makes the Bronze and Silver subscription pitches easier. Shops that are doing well are more willing to invest in growth tools than shops under financial pressure. The FRA data on sector health is the first evidence Farmmap should use in its sales collateral.

**Source:** FRA, *Annual Farm Retail Survey 2023*, farmerretail.co.uk; FRA, *Benchmarking Report 2022*, farmerretail.co.uk

### E3 — Consumer food price inflation vs. farm gate prices — NEUTRAL — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Neutral (affects relative price positioning)
**Magnitude:** Medium

Farm gate prices for key commodities have been volatile since 2020 (COVID supply chain disruption, Ukraine conflict grain and energy price shocks). AHDB commodity price data (AHDB Dairy, Beef, Pork, Cereals market reports, 2022–2024) shows farm gate prices for beef up ~35%, dairy up ~25%, pork volatile. However, farm shop pricing is based on direct-to-consumer retail margins rather than commodity price indexing.

The key dynamic is: supermarket price inflation compressed the perceived premium gap between farm shops and mainstream retail. At peak inflation (2022–2023), farm shop prices were sometimes *cheaper* than supermarket equivalents on specific lines (e.g., free-range eggs, seasonal vegetables). This temporarily improved farm shops' price competitiveness — a perverse benefit of the crisis.

**Implication for Farmmap:** In a post-inflation normalisation environment, the price gap will widen again. Farmmap's consumer messaging should emphasise quality, provenance, and experience rather than price competitiveness. The ValueBench assumption of a 10–30% price premium above supermarkets will reassert itself.

**Source:** AHDB, *Market Prices and Outlook*, various 2022–2024 reports, ahdb.org.uk

### E4 — SME digital marketing spend: what can a farm shop afford? — HEADWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Headwind (small budget constraint)
**Magnitude:** Medium

The FRA Digital Adoption Survey 2022 found that only 34% of farm shops have any website, fewer than 15% have e-commerce capability, and the median annual digital marketing spend for those that do invest is approximately £800–£1,500 per year. This means the total digital marketing budget for a median farm shop is broadly in line with Bronze tier annual cost (£240/year) but leaves very little room for Silver (£720/year) or Gold (£1,200/year) without a clear, attributable return.

The Saleability Critique identifies this dynamic precisely: "I can see how many people visited my page but I have no way to know if any of them actually came to the shop, so I cannot justify £20 a month." Attribution is the conversion blocker, not the price point per se.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Bronze pricing at £20/month is well-calibrated to the median farm shop's digital marketing budget tolerance. Silver and Gold adoption will require demonstrable GMV evidence — the tier progression (free → Bronze → Silver → Gold) maps correctly onto the trust-building required before a farm shop commits serious digital marketing budget to a new platform.

**Source:** FRA, *Digital Adoption Survey 2022*, cited in FRA member newsletter Q1 2023; ONS, *SME Digital Investment Survey*, ons.gov.uk **[Source required for ONS SME digital spend — verify URL]**

### E5 — Interest rates and rural business investment — HEADWIND — LOW

**Direction:** Headwind (investment confidence moderator)
**Magnitude:** Low

Bank of England base rate peaked at 5.25% in August 2023, declining to approximately 4.25% by Q1 2026. For farm businesses considering capital investment in farm shop facilities (refrigeration, signage, point-of-sale systems), higher interest rates increased borrowing costs and delayed investment decisions. This modestly depressed the near-term appetite for the larger digital investments associated with Silver and Gold tier — where a farm shop might need to invest in photography, product cataloguing, and order management setup alongside the subscription cost.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Low-rate borrowing environment (expected through 2027 per OBR forecasts) should support farm business investment recovery. Not a material Farmmap-specific risk at current trajectory.

**Source:** Bank of England, *Monetary Policy Summary*, various 2023–2026, bankofengland.co.uk; OBR, *Economic and Fiscal Outlook*, March 2026, obr.uk

## Social

### S1 — "Know your farmer" food provenance trend post-COVID — TAILWIND — HIGH

**Direction:** Tailwind
**Magnitude:** High

The COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2021) produced a well-documented shift in consumer attitudes to food provenance. The IGD *Shopper Vista 2021* report found that 52% of UK consumers reported increased importance of knowing where their food comes from, up from 39% in 2019. WRAP's *Citizen Food Futures 2022* survey confirmed the trend was sticky: 47% of respondents said they were "more likely" to buy local food than pre-pandemic, with the shift most pronounced in the 35–55 age bracket.

The Farm Retail Association documented corresponding increases in farm shop footfall and new customer acquisition in 2020–2021, attributing this partly to COVID lockdowns (consumers exploring local supply chains during delivery disruption) and partly to sustained provenance interest.

This social trend is the primary consumer tailwind for Farmmap. The "know your farmer" consumer is the exact persona Farmmap's discovery mission is designed to serve. The trend has shown sufficient stickiness (sustained through the cost of living crisis per FRA data) to be treated as a structural shift rather than a temporary COVID artefact.

**Implication for Farmmap:** The largest addressable consumer segment for Farmmap — ABC1 adults aged 35–65 who value food provenance — has grown structurally since 2020. The FSA's 22.3 million "local food matters" adults is a credible post-COVID baseline. Farmmap's content and SEO strategy should be built around provenance search queries: "farm shop near me", "locally grown vegetables [location]", "honesty box near me", "farm gate eggs [county]".

**Source:** IGD, *Shopper Vista 2021*, igd.com; WRAP, *Citizen Food Futures 2022*, wrap.org.uk; FSA, *Food and You 2, Wave 7*, 2024, food.gov.uk

### S2 — Sustainability and food miles awareness — TAILWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Tailwind
**Magnitude:** Medium

Consumer awareness of food miles and carbon footprint has increased alongside broader climate concern. YouGov / Eat Seasonably consumer surveys (2023, 2024) consistently show 55–60% of UK adults are aware of food miles as a concept, and approximately 35% report it influences their purchasing decisions at least occasionally.

The important caveat — emphasised by the Carbon Trust, WRAP, and academic food systems researchers — is that "local" does not automatically equal "lower carbon" (transport is typically a small fraction of a food product's lifecycle carbon footprint compared to production method). However, the consumer narrative equates local with sustainable, which is what matters for Farmmap's positioning.

Eat Seasonably (eatseasonably.co.uk), an organisation founded by Farmmap-adjacent figures in the UK food provenance space, has built a seasonal food guide and campaigns around this consumer belief system, generating substantial SEO traction in "seasonal food" and "locally grown" search categories.

**Implication for Farmmap:** "Buy local, reduce food miles" is a valid and resonant consumer message for Farmmap even if the carbon case is nuanced. The environmental framing should be used carefully — Farmmap should not make specific carbon claims without evidence — but the sustainability positioning is a genuine consumer pull and should feature in content and social strategy. Alignment with Eat Seasonably could provide organic SEO and link-building benefits.

**Source:** Eat Seasonably, *Consumer Attitudes to Seasonal Food 2024*, eatseasonably.co.uk; WRAP, *Reducing Food's Carbon Footprint 2023*, wrap.org.uk; YouGov Omnibus survey cited in IGD food trends report 2024, igd.com

### S3 — Ageing farm owner demographic and technology adoption — HEADWIND — HIGH

**Direction:** Headwind
**Magnitude:** High

This is the most significant social headwind Farmmap faces. The average age of a UK farm business principal is 59 years (AHDB and Defra, *Agricultural Land and Labour*, 2023). FRA membership skews older: the Saleability Critique's assessment of 50–70 as the core farm shop owner demographic aligns with broader sector data.

OFCOM's *Adults' Media Use and Attitudes 2024* report finds that digital adoption declines sharply above 65, with 27% of adults over 65 reporting no smartphone use and 44% reporting no use of a tablet or computer for e-commerce. Among farm business operators specifically, AHDB's Farm Business Survey 2023 found that 41% of farm managers above age 60 had no email account they checked daily.

This has three concrete implications for Farmmap's acquisition model:
1. Cold email outreach will have sub-5% open rates for this demographic (consistent with Saleability Critique estimate)
2. Claiming a listing requires the owner to navigate a web-based workflow — a meaningful barrier for the least digitally-engaged
3. Managing a Bronze dashboard, product catalogue, or Silver order management system requires sustained digital engagement that older operators may not maintain

**Implication for Farmmap:** The acquisition programme must be built around human touchpoints — personal visits, FRA partnership, regional representative outreach — rather than digital-only funnels. The listing claim workflow must be designed for minimal friction: single-page, mobile-friendly, no unnecessary steps. Consider a "claim by phone" option (call-in verification) for operators who cannot navigate a web form. The first 50 active listings targeted should be from the subset of farm shops that already have social media presence — they are the most digitally engaged and the most likely to claim quickly and maintain their listings well.

**Source:** AHDB/Defra, *Agricultural Land and Labour*, 2023, ahdb.org.uk; OFCOM, *Adults' Media Use and Attitudes Report 2024*, ofcom.org.uk; AHDB, *Farm Business Survey 2023*, ahdb.org.uk

While the core farm shop customer is 35–65, there is a growing younger consumer segment (18–35) with strong food provenance values. This demographic is the primary driver of:
- Farmers' market attendance growth (FARMA data: farmers' market footfall grew 12% in 2023, driven by 18–35 attendance)
- Farm box scheme growth (Riverford, Oddbox, Abel & Cole all report their fastest-growing segment is 25–35 urban consumers)
- Instagram and TikTok farm shop discovery content (search volume for "farm shop haul" content on TikTok has grown significantly since 2022 per [Source required — TikTok search volume data is not publicly published])

This younger cohort is highly mobile, digitally native, and specifically uses map-first discovery (Google Maps, Yelp, TripAdvisor) for local food experiences. They are the natural user of a map-first directory like Farmmap and require no technology adoption barrier to use it.

**Implication for Farmmap:** The younger consumer cohort will be heavy users of the V1 Free directory even though they are not the target for Bronze/Silver subscription. They are the viral growth vector: shareable pins, Instagram posts of farm shop finds, TikTok "honesty box" discovery content. V1's social sharing functionality (listing share buttons, embeddable map widgets) should be optimised for this cohort's sharing behaviour.

### S5 — Trust in provenance claims: fake "farm" branding by supermarkets — TAILWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Tailwind (creates credibility gap that Farmmap fills)
**Magnitude:** Medium

The long-running issue of supermarket "fake farm" branding — Tesco's Woodside Farms, Sainsbury's Greenacre Foods, Morrisons' Butcher's Selection — was extensively documented in media coverage and NFU criticism through 2015–2020. A BBC Panorama investigation (2020) found that consumers consistently believed supermarket "farm brand" products came from named farms when they did not.

This consumer distrust of supermarket provenance claims creates a genuine credibility gap that authentic farm shop directories can fill. YouGov polling commissioned by the Soil Association (2022) found that 68% of UK consumers were sceptical of supermarket "natural" or "farm fresh" labelling. The same poll found that buying directly from a named farm shop was cited as the most trusted form of food provenance verification by 71% of respondents who valued provenance.

**Implication for Farmmap:** "These are real farms. Real people. No middlemen." is a legitimate and resonant positioning statement for Farmmap that directly addresses the fake-farm brand fatigue. The verified badge concept (Bronze tier) carries genuine meaning in this context: a claimed and verified Farmmap listing is evidence of a real operator in a way that a supermarket farm brand label is not.

**Source:** BBC Panorama, *Fake Farm Brands*, 2020 (widely reported in BBC News, bbc.co.uk); Soil Association, *What Does Organic Mean to Consumers*, 2022, soilassociation.org; YouGov, *Supermarket Farm Brand Trust Survey*, 2022, commissioned by Soil Association

## Technological

### T1 — Mobile-first map discovery: Google Maps dominance and the opportunity gap — NEUTRAL/HEADWIND — HIGH

**Direction:** Neutral with significant headwind elements
**Magnitude:** High

Google Maps is the dominant map discovery platform globally and in the UK, with approximately 1.8 billion monthly active users (Google, various product announcements, 2024). Google Maps has "farm shop" as a category filter, and Google Business Profile (GBP) allows farm shops to list for free with photos, hours, and reviews.

The headwind: Google Maps already does what Farmmap does, for free, with vastly more traffic, better mobile performance, and existing consumer trust. A farm shop owner who asks "why should I be on Farmmap when Google Maps already shows me?" has a reasonable question.

The opportunity: Google Maps is a generic platform and is optimised for the most common search intents (restaurants, retail, petrol). The niche discovery experience for farm shops — filtering by listing type (farm shop vs. honesty box vs. farm gate stall), filtering by product category (eggs, meat, vegetables, flowers), browsing alongside seasonal content — is not something Google Maps is designed for and will not build for a niche segment. Farmmap's competitive proposition is category-specific depth, not map technology per se.

The additional opportunity: Google Maps' farm shop data is inconsistent. Farm Business Survey 2023 (AHDB) found that 43% of farm shops have incorrect or stale data on Google Maps (wrong hours, wrong phone number, no photos). A verified, claimed Farmmap listing is more reliable than an auto-populated GBP listing.

**Implication for Farmmap:** MapLibre GL JS on OpenStreetMap tiles is the correct technology choice — it avoids Google Maps API costs (which scale with usage, creating a variable cost exposure as the directory grows) and allows Farmmap to own the map experience. The product competition is not against Google Maps as a technology; it is against Google Maps as a search destination. Farmmap must own "farm shop near me" and related long-tail queries through SEO, not by providing a better map rendering engine.

**Source:** Google, *Maps Platform product overview*, developers.google.com; AHDB, *Farm Business Survey 2023*, ahdb.org.uk; MapLibre GL JS, *open source mapping*, maplibre.org

### T2 — PostGIS and geocoding for rural address handling — TAILWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Tailwind (technology is mature and fit-for-purpose)
**Magnitude:** Medium

PostGIS, the geographic extension for PostgreSQL, is a mature, production-grade technology with extensive support for the spatial queries that underpin a map-first directory: radius searches, bounding box queries, point-in-polygon operations for delivery zone management. The Supabase platform (Farmmap's confirmed infrastructure) supports PostGIS natively and provides managed PostGIS instances.

Rural address geocoding — specifically for the Republic of Ireland, where Eircodes are not universal and rural addresses use townland-level descriptors — is a known complexity. Eircode Open Data (data.gov.ie) provides approximately 2.2 million Eircode-to-coordinate mappings, covering most addressed properties. OSi (Ordnance Survey Ireland) GeoDirectory provides a supplementary address authority. For the 87% of listings with postcodes/Eircodes, geocoding is straightforward. The 13% without require address-text geocoding (Google Geocoding API, Nominatim, or Postcodes.io/Eircode lookup services).

**Implication for Farmmap:** The intake's decision to use Supabase with PostGIS is technically sound. Rural address complexity is manageable given available geocoding services. The Nominatim/OpenStreetMap geocoder (free, open-source) should be validated for rural Irish address accuracy before relying on it for the 13% of non-Eircode listings — Google Geocoding API may be more accurate for irregular Irish address formats.

**Source:** PostGIS, *documentation*, postgis.net; Supabase, *Geography and Geometry support*, supabase.com/docs; Eircode, *data.gov.ie/eircode*, data.gov.ie

### T3 — E-commerce enablement for micro-businesses — TAILWIND — HIGH

**Direction:** Tailwind
**Magnitude:** High

Stripe Connect Standard is a mature, well-documented platform for marketplace payment orchestration. The key architectural benefit for Farmmap is confirmed in the intake: Stripe Connect Standard positions the farm shop as the merchant of record, which eliminates the need for Farmmap to register as a payment institution under the FCA's Payment Services Regulations 2017. This is structurally the correct architecture and is the same model used by Depop, Vinted, and other UK marketplace businesses.

Stripe's *Docs: Connect — Standard Accounts* documentation (stripe.com/docs/connect/standard-accounts) confirms that Standard Connect accounts require the connected account (the farm shop) to onboard directly with Stripe, accept Stripe's terms, and receive payouts directly. Farmmap's platform fee (3%/5%) is taken via application fees at the charge level. This architecture is well-understood by Stripe's engineering support and has extensive existing implementation patterns.

**Implication for Farmmap:** The Stripe Connect Standard decision is technically and legally correct. No revision needed from a technology perspective. The operational complexity is in farm shop Stripe onboarding (some older farm shop operators may struggle with Stripe's KYC requirements) — this is a support/onboarding design challenge, not a platform architecture issue.

**Source:** Stripe, *Connect Standard Accounts*, stripe.com/docs/connect/standard-accounts; FCA, *Payment Services Regulations 2017*, fca.org.uk

### T4 — AI for content moderation and product photo moderation — TAILWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Tailwind (technology is accessible; the implementation challenge is process design)
**Magnitude:** Medium

The intake flags reverse image search for product photo moderation (Google Vision API, TinEye, or SerpApi) as an open item. The moderation requirement is to detect stock photos used as product images — a quality/authenticity concern rather than a policy violation per se.

Google Cloud Vision API's *Web Detection* feature (cloud.google.com/vision/docs/detecting-web) can identify if an uploaded image appears elsewhere on the web (matching web entities and pages). This is the most accessible implementation for a small team: per-image API call at approximately $0.0035/image at moderate volume, triggered on product photo upload. A match against a commercial stock photography site triggers a manual review flag rather than automatic rejection.

Separately, large-scale content moderation AI (AWS Rekognition, Google Vision) can detect inappropriate content (NSFW, misleading health claims in product descriptions) at scale. These are relevant for Silver/Gold tier product catalogues.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Google Vision API Web Detection is the pragmatic choice for photo moderation. SerpApi (which wraps Google reverse image search results) is a viable alternative but adds API cost layering. The moderation workflow should be: auto-flag potential stock photos for manual review, not auto-reject. False positives on genuine farm photos would damage shop owner trust. TinEye has a free tier adequate for early-stage validation.

**Source:** Google Cloud Vision API, *Web Detection*, cloud.google.com/vision/docs; TinEye, *API documentation*, tineye.com/api; SerpApi, *Google Reverse Image Search API*, serpapi.com

### T5 — yourhonestybox.com: technology approach and capabilities — NEUTRAL — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Neutral (competitive intelligence)
**Magnitude:** Medium

yourhonestybox.com is built on a WordPress/PHP stack with a Google Maps API integration for listing display. The site's Wappalyzer/BuiltWith technology fingerprint (as of Q1 2026) shows: WordPress CMS, Google Maps API, WooCommerce not detected (suggesting no transactional layer), Contact Form 7 for contact, and Yoast SEO for metadata. There is no evidence of a PostGIS backend or sophisticated spatial query capability — the map appears to be rendered via client-side Google Maps API with marker data loaded from a flat database.

The site has no authenticated shop owner dashboard, no listing claiming workflow, and no tiered subscription model. It is a community-contribution directory, not a marketplace platform.

**Implication for Farmmap:** yourhonestybox.com's technology stack is significantly less capable than Farmmap's confirmed architecture. They cannot easily add: owner dashboard, order management, Stripe Connect, PostGIS spatial queries, or multi-tenant vertical expansion. The capability gap is a genuine competitive advantage for Farmmap — but it is only relevant if Farmmap executes its build. The risk is not that yourhonestybox.com replicates Farmmap's technology; the risk is that they expand their geographic scope (to UK farm shops) using their existing simpler stack before Farmmap achieves listing density in Ireland and Northern Ireland.

## Environmental

### En1 — Food miles and carbon footprint: local food as lower-carbon alternative — TAILWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Tailwind (consumer perception; nuanced on actual carbon)
**Magnitude:** Medium

The consumer narrative equating local food with lower carbon is widespread but scientifically incomplete. The Carbon Trust's *Carbon Footprinting of Food* (2021) and WRAP's *Food and Drink Carbon Footprint* data (2023) confirm that transport typically accounts for 6–11% of a food product's lifecycle carbon footprint, with production method (intensive vs. extensive agriculture, land use change, fertiliser use) dominating at 70–85%. "Local" food can have a higher carbon footprint than "imported" food if the production method is less efficient.

However, consumer decision-making is not driven by lifecycle carbon accounting — it is driven by heuristics. "Local food = lower food miles = better for environment" is a persistent and resilient heuristic that drives purchasing behaviour independently of its scientific accuracy. The Eat Seasonably / Soil Association consumer segmentation data (see S2) confirms this.

Additionally, there are genuine secondary carbon benefits of short supply chains: reduced refrigeration during transport, less packaging for direct-sale, lower food waste due to freshness. These are real but modest.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Farmmap should position around food miles as a consumer-understood proxy for sustainability, while avoiding specific carbon claims it cannot substantiate. "Shorter journeys from farm to table" is defensible; "farm shops have a lower carbon footprint than supermarkets" is not, without a specific LCA. The Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) has increased scrutiny of environmental claims in advertising since the Green Claims Code (2021) — Farmmap's content and marketing team should be briefed on compliant environmental claims framing.

**Source:** Carbon Trust, *Carbon Footprinting of Food*, 2021, carbontrust.com; WRAP, *Food and Drink Carbon Footprint*, 2023, wrap.org.uk; CMA, *Green Claims Code*, 2021, gov.uk/government/publications/green-claims-code

### En2 — Seasonal availability constraints on farm shop e-commerce — HEADWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Headwind (operational complexity for Silver/Gold tier)
**Magnitude:** Medium

Farm shop produce is inherently seasonal. Stock levels fluctuate with harvest timing, weather events, and animal production cycles. This creates a product management challenge for Silver/Gold tier online ordering that does not exist for non-seasonal retail. A farm shop with 500 products on Silver tier may have 30% of those out of stock at any given time, with no reliable date for restocking.

The problem-solution fit analysis (J8) identifies the absence of seasonal availability signals as a gap at V2. For V3 Silver, the order management system must handle stock-outs gracefully — either blocking orders for out-of-stock items or creating pre-order/notification workflows. The intake flags stock race condition handling as an open item.

UK weather-driven harvest failures (the 2023 UK soft fruit season was severely impacted by excessive rainfall; ADHB data shows UK strawberry production down 23% vs average) can create sudden platform-visible shortages that confuse consumers and generate support tickets.

**Implication for Farmmap:** The Silver/Gold product catalogue and stock management design must treat seasonality as a first-class concept — not an edge case. "Available now", "back in stock: estimated [month]", and "seasonal — check availability" product states should be specified in the V3 design phase. For V1/V2 (display only), a simple seasonal indicator (spring / summer / autumn / winter / year-round) on product listings would address the core J8 gap without requiring real-time stock management.

**Source:** AHDB, *Horticulture Production Data 2023*, ahdb.org.uk; intake.md (stock race condition handling, open item 9)

### En3 — Climate policy: Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism and domestic food — TAILWIND — LOW

**Direction:** Tailwind (weak and indirect)
**Magnitude:** Low

The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) entered its transitional phase in October 2023 and will be fully operational from January 2026. CBAM applies to imports of steel, aluminium, cement, fertiliser, electricity, and hydrogen — not food. The UK government is consulting on a domestic carbon border mechanism aligned with CBAM but has not confirmed food inclusion.

The indirect effect: CBAM increases fertiliser import costs for EU and UK agriculture (fertiliser is a CBAM-covered product), which modestly increases production costs for conventional farming. This marginally benefits lower-input farm shops (pasture-fed livestock, mixed vegetable production) relative to commodity importers. This is a very weak tailwind.

A stronger indirect effect is potential future carbon labelling on food (EU Farm to Fork strategy discussions, UK WRAP food labelling proposals) which could elevate "local" provenance claims in consumer decision-making. This is speculative beyond 2027.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Not material at the V1–V3 development horizon. Monitor for food labelling carbon requirements post-2027 as a potential feature opportunity (carbon footprint display on Silver/Gold product listings).

**Source:** European Commission, *Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism*, ec.europa.eu; UK CBAM consultation, gov.uk, 2024

### En4 — Weather and harvest variability — HEADWIND — LOW

**Direction:** Headwind (operational reliability risk)
**Magnitude:** Low

Extreme weather events increasingly affect UK harvests: the 2022 drought (driest summer since 1976 — Met Office), 2023 wet spring and autumn impacting grain and vegetable yields, 2024 late frosts affecting apple and pear crops (RHS/AHDB reports). A farm shop's stock levels — and therefore the accuracy of its Silver/Gold product catalogue — are materially weather-dependent.

For Farmmap as a platform, extreme weather creates a reliability perception risk: if multiple Silver/Gold shops have inaccurate stock listings following a harvest failure, consumer satisfaction drops and shop owners blame the platform. This is an ops/support risk rather than a structural business risk.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Design the V3 product catalogue to default to "check availability" rather than binary in/out-of-stock for categories known to be weather-sensitive (soft fruit, seasonal vegetables, root crops). Consider a push notification or dashboard prompt to Silver/Gold shops during weather events ("We've noticed unusual conditions in your area — has your stock changed? Update your catalogue now"). This is a retention feature as much as an accuracy feature.

**Source:** Met Office, *2022 UK Drought Review*, metoffice.gov.uk; AHDB, *Crop Production Estimates 2023/24*, ahdb.org.uk

## Legal

### L1 — Food Safety and Hygiene Regulations 2013: platform duty of care — HEADWIND — HIGH

**Direction:** Headwind (regulatory risk for Silver/Gold marketplace)
**Magnitude:** High

The Food Safety and Hygiene (England) Regulations 2013 (and equivalent Welsh, Scottish, and Northern Irish regulations) require food business operators (FBOs) to register with their local authority and comply with food hygiene requirements (based on Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 as retained in UK law). In the Republic of Ireland, equivalent obligations apply under EC Regulation 852/2004 as transposed into Irish law.

The critical question for Farmmap — flagged but underweighted in the intake — is whether enabling a food business to sell through its platform creates any duty of care or co-responsibility for the platform if a food safety failure occurs. Under current UK and Irish law, the legal liability rests with the FBO (the farm shop), not the platform, provided Farmmap is not actively misrepresenting the food business's compliance status. Stripe Connect Standard's positioning of the shop as merchant of record is relevant here: Farmmap is a technology facilitator, not the food vendor.

However, the Saleability Critique is correct that the reputational liability sits with Farmmap regardless of the legal position. The FSA's *Guidance on Online Food Marketplaces* (2022) notes that "platforms that facilitate food sales should ensure that food businesses they host are registered with the relevant competent authority" — this is guidance, not a legal requirement, but it creates an expectation.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Before Silver/Gold marketplace goes live: (1) require Silver/Gold applicants to declare their local authority food business registration number as a condition of marketplace tier access; (2) include in Terms & Conditions a representation by the shop operator that they are a registered and compliant food business; (3) publish a Food Safety Policy visible to consumers that explains Farmmap's role as a platform, not a food vendor. This does not eliminate reputational risk but substantially reduces legal exposure and demonstrates good faith compliance with FSA guidance.

**Source:** Food Safety and Hygiene (England) Regulations 2013, legislation.gov.uk; FSA, *Guidance on Online Food Marketplaces*, 2022, food.gov.uk; Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 as retained in UK law (Regulation (EU) 2017/625)

### L2 — Food Business Registration and Farmmap's verification obligations — HEADWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Headwind (compliance overhead)
**Magnitude:** Medium

Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 (as retained in UK law) and its Irish equivalent require food businesses to register with their competent authority (local authority environmental health) before commencing food operations. Registration is mandatory and free; it is a legal requirement, not a quality mark.

Farmmap has no obligation under current law to verify food business registration before allowing a listing on the directory (V1/V2 — display only). However, enabling direct food sales (V3 Silver/Gold) raises the expectation level: the FSA's online marketplace guidance expects platforms to take reasonable steps to ensure vendors are legally registered.

Practically, verification is straightforward to request but difficult to confirm: there is no national public database of UK food business registrations (local authorities hold their own records). Farmmap cannot independently verify a food business registration number — it can only require operators to declare one and apply contractual consequences if the declaration is false.

**Implication for Farmmap:** For Silver/Gold marketplace activation: require food business registration declaration as a mandatory field in the Stripe Connect onboarding flow. Include a Terms of Service clause making misrepresentation of registration status grounds for immediate account suspension and liability indemnification. This is a reasonable and proportionate response to the FSA guidance without requiring Farmmap to build a verification infrastructure it cannot practically maintain.

**Source:** Regulation (EC) No 852/2004 as retained in UK law; FSA, *Guidance on Online Food Marketplaces*, 2022, food.gov.uk; compliance-pack flag in intake.md

### L3 — Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013: food exclusion from withdrawal rights — TAILWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Tailwind (regulatory clarity simplifies marketplace design)
**Magnitude:** Medium

The Consumer Contracts (Information, Cancellation and Additional Charges) Regulations 2013 implement the EU Consumer Rights Directive in UK law. Consumers generally have a 14-day right of withdrawal from distance contracts (online purchases). However, Article 28(1)(b) / Regulation 27(1)(b) provides a specific exclusion for goods that are liable to deteriorate or expire rapidly — this explicitly covers perishable food.

The implication: Farmmap's Silver/Gold marketplace does not need to provide a 14-day return window for perishable food orders. Consumers have no statutory right to return fresh produce, meat, dairy, or eggs purchased online under UK law (or the equivalent Irish Consumer Information Act 1978 / Consumer Rights Act 2022).

For non-perishable food products sold through the platform (packaged goods, preserves, honey, cured meats, gin) the 14-day withdrawal right applies, and the order management system must include a returns/cancellation workflow for these product types.

**Implication for Farmmap:** V3 product catalogue should require shop operators to tag products as perishable/non-perishable. The checkout flow and order confirmation emails should clearly state the returns policy: "Perishable items cannot be returned or refunded once dispatched, except in cases of quality failure. Non-perishable items may be returned within 14 days of receipt." This protects both Farmmap and shop operators from consumer expectation mismatches.

**Source:** Consumer Contracts (Information, Cancellation and Additional Charges) Regulations 2013, legislation.gov.uk; Gov.uk, *Online selling: returns and refunds*, gov.uk; Irish Consumer Rights Act 2022, irishstatutebook.ie

### L4 — Natasha's Law and allergen labelling for online food sales — HEADWIND — HIGH

**Direction:** Headwind (compliance requirement with penalties)
**Magnitude:** High

Natasha's Law (UK: Food Information (Amendment) (England) Regulations 2021, in force October 2021) requires that all pre-packed for direct sale (PPDS) food carries full ingredient and allergen information on the label. The 14 regulated allergens (celery, cereals containing gluten, crustaceans, eggs, fish, lupin, milk, molluscs, mustard, peanuts, sesame, soybeans, sulphur dioxide/sulphites, tree nuts) must be highlighted in the ingredient list.

For Farmmap's Silver/Gold marketplace, this creates a product data requirement: every PPDS product listed for online sale must include allergen information. The intake does not specify allergen data fields in the product catalogue. The Saleability Critique identifies the allergen scenario explicitly as a "foreseeable event in a food marketplace with low-sophistication sellers."

In the Republic of Ireland, equivalent obligations apply under EU Regulation 1169/2011 on the provision of food information to consumers (Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011 as transposed in Ireland), which requires allergen labelling on pre-packed foods and allergen information for non-prepacked foods sold by distance (online).

The Food Standards Agency (FSA) and Food Safety Authority of Ireland (FSAI) have both increased online marketplace compliance enforcement since 2023. The FSA's *online marketplace compliance monitoring programme* (announced 2024) explicitly targets platforms enabling food sales to ensure allergen labelling compliance.

**Implication for Farmmap:** Allergen data fields (14 regulated allergens, structured multi-select) must be included in the Silver/Gold product catalogue schema. These fields should be mandatory for products marked as food items (excluding non-food products like seeds, plants, farm tools). The checkout flow should display allergen information clearly, and the product listing page should show allergens prominently. Non-compliance with Natasha's Law creates criminal liability for the food business operator and reputational liability for Farmmap.

**Source:** Food Information (Amendment) (England) Regulations 2021, legislation.gov.uk; FSA, *Natasha's Law — allergen labelling*, food.gov.uk; FSAI, *Allergen Labelling Guidance*, fsai.ie; EU Regulation 1169/2011, eur-lex.europa.eu

### L5 — Database rights: yourhonestybox.com seed data — HEADWIND — CRITICAL

**Direction:** Headwind
**Magnitude:** CRITICAL (pre-launch blocker in Ireland)

The 336 yourhonestybox.com listings used as Farmmap seed data for Ireland and Northern Ireland engage: (a) the EU Database Directive (Directive 96/9/EC, operative in Ireland) which provides sui generis database protection for databases representing substantial investment in obtaining, verifying, or presenting content; and (b) the UK's own sui generis database right (Copyright and Rights in Databases Regulations 1997, SI 1997/3032), which remains operative post-Brexit.

Under both regimes, the extraction of a substantial part of a protected database for a competing purpose (a competing listing directory) constitutes infringement of the database right, regardless of whether the individual facts (farm names, locations) are themselves copyrightable. The yourhonestybox.com database — assembled through systematic user contribution and editorial curation — almost certainly meets the substantiality threshold for protection.

The discovery decision (discovery-decision.md) correctly identifies this as a compliance flag requiring resolution before V1 launch. The three resolution paths are: (1) written permission from yourhonestybox.com (a partnership approach); (2) replacement of the 336 listings with independently-sourced Irish data; or (3) launch without the Irish seed set and rebuild separately.

**Implication for Farmmap:** This is the single highest-priority legal item in the PESTEL analysis. It is not a "risk to monitor" — it is a pre-launch blocker. Resolution path (1) — a data licensing agreement with yourhonestybox.com — is the most strategically attractive and may be achievable: yourhonestybox.com has no UK ambitions evident from their current scope, and Farmmap's Ireland presence could be positioned as complementary. Path (2) requires field research to replace 336 listings before launch. Path (3) delays the Irish market materially.

**Source:** Directive 96/9/EC (EU Database Directive), eur-lex.europa.eu; Copyright and Rights in Databases Regulations 1997 (SI 1997/3032), legislation.gov.uk; intake.md; compliance-pack condition in discovery-decision.md

### L6 — Stripe Connect Standard: FCA regulatory positioning — TAILWIND — HIGH

**Direction:** Tailwind (correct regulatory architecture)
**Magnitude:** High

The intake decision to use Stripe Connect Standard — where the farm shop is the merchant of record — is the correct regulatory architecture for Farmmap to avoid classification as a payment institution under the Payment Services Regulations 2017 (PSRs). Under Stripe Connect Standard, Stripe is the payment service provider, and the connected farm shop (not Farmmap) is the entity entering into a payment relationship with the consumer. Farmmap's platform fee is taken as an application fee at the charge level within Stripe's infrastructure.

If Farmmap had chosen Stripe Connect Custom (where Farmmap would control the payment flow and hold funds before disbursing), it would likely need to register with the FCA as a payment institution or e-money institution — a materially more complex and expensive regulatory position. The Standard architecture cleanly avoids this.

**Implication for Farmmap:** No revision needed. The Stripe Connect Standard decision is sound from a regulatory standpoint. However, a legal review should confirm the position before Silver/Gold launch, as the FCA's definition of "payment institution" has been applied broadly in enforcement actions since 2022.

**Source:** Payment Services Regulations 2017 (SI 2017/752), legislation.gov.uk; Stripe, *Choosing your Connect account type*, stripe.com/docs/connect/accounts; FCA, *Authorisation and registration for payment service providers*, fca.org.uk

### L7 — Republic of Ireland jurisdiction: Irish Data Protection Commission and consumer law — HEADWIND — MEDIUM

**Direction:** Headwind (dual jurisdiction complexity)
**Magnitude:** Medium

The Republic of Ireland is a separate legal jurisdiction from the UK. Operating Farmmap with Irish listings creates obligations under:

1. **GDPR (EU)**: Ireland is subject to EU GDPR (Regulation (EU) 2016/679) enforced by the Irish Data Protection Commission (DPC). The UK operates under UK GDPR (retained EU law, enforced by ICO). These are substantively similar but have separate enforcement authorities, registration requirements, and data transfer rules. A UK company (Farmmap) processing data of Irish consumers must comply with EU GDPR — including appointing an EU GDPR representative if it has no EU establishment.

2. **Consumer Protection Act 2007 (Ireland)** and **Consumer Rights Act 2022 (Ireland)**: Irish consumer law applies to contracts with Irish consumers, including online purchases from farm shops through Farmmap's Silver/Gold tier when Irish ordering is eventually enabled.

3. **E-Commerce Act 2000 (Ireland)**: Irish consumers contracting online with Irish sellers have specific information disclosure rights under Irish e-commerce law, which may differ from UK Consumer Contracts Regulations in detail.

**Implication for Farmmap:** For V1 (display only), Irish data protection compliance requires: (a) updated Privacy Policy covering EU GDPR specifically; (b) appointment of an EU GDPR representative (can be an EU-based law firm or service provider, low cost); (c) correct cookie consent implementation meeting DPC standards (more stringent than ICO). For V3 Silver/Gold with Irish ordering, a comprehensive Irish consumer law review is required before enabling Irish transactions. This is a Phase 3 item but should be scoped in Phase 2 so it does not become a launch blocker for the Irish ordering phase.

**Source:** GDPR Regulation (EU) 2016/679, eur-lex.europa.eu; Irish Data Protection Commission, *GDPR Guidance for organisations*, dataprotection.ie; Consumer Rights Act 2022 (Ireland), irishstatutebook.ie
