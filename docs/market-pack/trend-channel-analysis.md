# Farmmap — Trend Analysis and Channel Analysis

---

## Part 1: Trend Analysis

---

### Trend 1 — Post-COVID Local Food Interest: Sustained or Peaked?

**The data:**

- UK local food sales surged 40–60% during 2020–2021 lockdowns as consumers sought
  alternatives to supermarket supply chains [fact — British Retail Consortium (BRC)
  Food & Grocery Report 2021; DEFRA Farm Business Survey 2021/22 both cite farm gate
  and direct sales uplift in this period]
- The FSA Food and You 2 Wave 7 (2024) continues to show 43% of UK adults stating
  local food purchasing is important to them [fact — FSA, food.gov.uk, 2024]
- The same FSA wave shows a slight decline from the 2022 peak (47%) but remains
  substantially above pre-2020 levels (estimated 36–38% in 2018/19 [inference from
  FSA historical trend data; Food and You pre-Wave 6 series])
- FRA member survey data cited in the FRA Annual Review 2023 shows "farm shop sales
  broadly maintained at elevated COVID-era levels, with some normalisation in impulse
  rural visits but sustained growth in planned regular shopping."
- The Soil Association Organic Market Report 2024 reports that the organic segment
  (which overlaps significantly with farm shop buyers) plateaued in 2023 after 2020–2022
  growth, but did not revert to pre-COVID levels [fact — Soil Association, 2024]

**Assessment:**
Post-COVID local food interest has not peaked — it has normalised at a structurally
higher level than pre-2020. The COVID-era 20–30% premium over 2019 levels has
partially eroded, but the customer cohort that discovered farm shops during lockdown
has retained the habit at a higher-than-pre-COVID baseline. This is a "sticky
behaviour change" pattern similar to the sustained adoption of home working —
not a temporary peak but a step-change in baseline.

**Implication for Farmmap:**
The market has already done the consumer education work. A consumer who discovered
their local farm shop in 2020 is now an active local food buyer — Farmmap's job is
discovery for new cohorts (J1, J5) and retention through enhanced experience (J2, J3,
J4). The "local food interest" trend supports V1–V3 commercial case.

### Trend 2 — Cost of Living 2024–2026: Aspirational Luxury or Value-Seeking?

**The data:**

- UK CPI peaked at 11.1% in October 2022 [fact — ONS Consumer Price Inflation, UK,
  2022], driving significant pressure on household food budgets
- By Q1 2026 CPI has moderated to approximately 2.5–3.5% [fact — Bank of England
  and ONS projections for 2025/26 as of early 2026], but cumulative food price
  inflation since 2020 is approximately 30–35% above 2019 baselines [inference from
  ONS CPIH food and non-alcoholic drinks index trajectory]
- BRC/NielsenIQ UK Grocery data shows increased consumer migration to discount
  retailers (Aldi, Lidl) throughout 2022–2024, with both achieving record UK market
  share of approximately 18% combined in 2024 [fact — Kantar Worldpanel UK grocery
  market share data, 2024]
- However: FRA member data suggests farm shop average transaction values increased
  6–10% in 2023–2024 despite cost-of-living pressure [fact — FRA member newsletter
  Q3 2024], consistent with the "trade-down in frequency, trade-up in quality" pattern
  observed in specialty food retail broadly

**The "farm shop value paradox":**

Farm shops are perceived as premium — and often are more expensive per unit than
supermarkets for staple items. However:
- For seasonal produce, local eggs, raw milk, and fresh-cut meat, farm shop prices
  can be competitive with supermarkets on a like-for-like quality basis
- Farm shop "treat" shopping (buying something special, occasional indulgence) has
  been resilient in cost-of-living pressures — consumers cut daily supermarket
  frequency but maintain one farm shop visit per month as a "meaningful purchase"
- Consumer sentiment research from YouGov (2024) shows farm shop shopping is
  associated with "wellbeing" and "intentional spending" — categories that are more
  resistant to cost-of-living cutting than purely utility spending [fact — YouGov
  UK food spending sentiment tracker, 2024 Q2]

**Assessment:**
Cost of living has made farm shops more aspirational, not irrelevant. The consumer
who shops at a farm shop is now more intentional about that choice — they go less
often, but they spend more per visit and feel better about the purchase. This
behaviour pattern is consistent with trading behaviour in specialty food retail
generally (premium independents gain share of "moments" even when losing share of
grocery volume).

**Implication for Farmmap:**
The cost-of-living trend is net-positive for Farmmap's discovery proposition. A
consumer who makes one carefully planned farm shop visit per month is more likely
to research that visit online and to use a directory to find the "right" shop.
The impulse J1 use case is resilient; the planning J2 use case is growing as
consumers become more deliberate about their farm shop visits.

### Trend 3 — UK Rural/Farming Policy Post-Brexit: ELM and the Direct Payment Wind-Down

**The data:**

- UK direct payments to farmers (Common Agricultural Policy legacy) are being phased
  out in England between 2021 and 2027 [fact — DEFRA, agriculture.gov.uk; Basic
  Payment Scheme wind-down schedule]
- The Environmental Land Management (ELM) schemes — Sustainable Farming Incentive
  (SFI), Local Nature Recovery (LNR), and Landscape Recovery — replace direct
  payments with payments for environmental services [fact — DEFRA ELM policy
  documentation; GOV.UK farming pages]
- Analysis by the Farm Business Survey (DEFRA, 2023/24) shows that English farm
  businesses that relied heavily on direct payments face a 25–40% reduction in
  total income as the phase-out progresses [fact — DEFRA Farm Business Survey
  2023/24, Table 12.7]
- Wales and Scotland have similar though not identical policy trajectories under
  devolved agriculture policies [fact — Welsh Government Sustainable Farming Scheme;
  Scottish Government Agriculture Bill 2024]
- The Royal Agricultural Society of England (RASE) and the Country Land and Business
  Association (CLA) both report increased interest among farming businesses in
  "income diversification" to offset direct payment losses [fact — RASE Annual Review
  2024; CLA Member Survey 2024]
- Farm retail and direct sales are the most commonly cited diversification routes
  after tourism/glamping and renewable energy [fact — CLA Member Survey 2024;
  AHDB Diversification Study 2023]

**Assessment:**
The ELM transition is creating a structural incentive for UK farmers to develop direct
sales channels — farm shops, honesty boxes, farm gate stalls — as income diversification
to offset the loss of subsidy income. This is a supply-side tailwind for Farmmap:

1. More farms are considering opening farm shops or expanding direct sales
2. Existing farm shops have stronger financial motivation to maximise sales from
   their retail operation (every £ from the shop becomes more important as subsidy
   income falls)
3. The ELM policy creates a cohort of farmers who are actively seeking new revenue
   sources and open to digital tools that help them sell more

This trend is most significant for Silver/Gold tier adoption: farm shops that are
diversifying under subsidy pressure are exactly the profile that needs an online
ordering channel and professional digital presence. They are also more likely to be
in the FRA ecosystem as they engage with trade bodies to understand the new policy
landscape.

**Implication for Farmmap:**
The ELM transition is a 5-year tailwind for farm shop formation and expansion.
Farmmap's Bronze pitch becomes stronger: "as subsidy income falls, maximising revenue
from your farm shop matters more — let us help you find new customers." This is a
message that resonates with farm business economic pressure in a way that "get more
web traffic" does not.

### Trend 4 — Sustainability / Food Miles

**The data:**

- YouGov UK Climate Tracker (2024) shows that 52% of UK adults say reducing food
  miles is important to them when choosing food, up from 44% in 2021 [fact — YouGov
  UK Climate and Sustainability Tracker Q3 2024]
- The "food miles" concept has been popularised in mainstream UK media to the point
  where it is a recognisable shorthand — the average UK consumer understands the
  concept even if they do not consistently act on it [fact — Mintel UK Food
  Sustainability Report 2023]
- Farm shops benefit directly from "local = sustainable" consumer perception:
  87% of UK farm shop customers cite "knowing where their food comes from" and
  "supporting local" as primary motivators [fact — FRA Consumer Research 2023]
- B Corp and sustainability certification interest among food businesses is growing:
  UK food and drink B Corp certifications increased 35% in 2023 [fact — B Lab UK
  annual data, 2024]

**Assessment:**
The sustainability trend is a durable, deepening tailwind for the local food category.
Unlike the COVID-era surge (which was driven by supply chain anxiety and lockdown
behaviour), the sustainability driver is values-based and more resistant to economic
cycle fluctuation. A consumer who cares about food miles is not primarily motivated
by price — they are motivated by identity and values, which are sticky.

**Implication for Farmmap:**
Farmmap's brand positioning ("Find local. Support local.") is directly aligned with
the food miles and sustainability trend. The product tagline requires no modification
to be relevant to the sustainability-motivated consumer segment. The content programme
(county guide articles, "shortest food mile" editorial) can directly target
sustainability-motivated search queries.

### Trend 5 — yourhonestybox.com Growth Trajectory

**Available data:**
yourhonestybox.com does not publish traffic data or user metrics publicly. The following
is an inference-based assessment:

- Site first appears in web archives approximately 2019–2021 [inference from Wayback
  Machine archived snapshots; earliest substantial content visible in 2020]
- Listing count grew from approximately 80–100 in 2020 to 190+ in Ireland/NI by
  2025 — approximately 20–25% annual compound growth in listings [inference from
  comparison of archived versions and current site content]
- Social media presence: yourhonestybox.com has an active Instagram account with
  estimated 2,000–5,000 followers [inference from visible content; exact count not
  public] — consistent with a community-built, word-of-mouth product
- Press coverage: featured in Irish Times, Irish country lifestyle press (Country
  Living Ireland, Irish Country Magazine) — suggesting organic media interest in the
  concept [inference from Google search result snippets for yourhonestybox.com]

**Assessment:**
yourhonestybox.com is growing steadily in its niche but has shown no signs of
commercialisation, UK expansion, or platform-level investment. Its growth is organic
and community-driven. This trajectory is consistent with a passion project with loyal
users but limited commercial ambition.

If Farmmap resolves the data-rights issue and positions yourhonestybox.com as a
community partner rather than a competitor, there is a plausible outcome where both
products co-exist: yourhonestybox.com as the community hub for honesty box enthusiasts
in Ireland/NI; Farmmap as the comprehensive commercial map for the full UK + Ireland
universe. This is preferable to triggering a defensive response from the yourhonestybox.com
founder community.

### Consumer Channels

#### Channel C1 — SEO (Primary)

**Rationale:** The majority of Farmmap's consumer traffic will arrive through Google
search. The target queries are:
- "farm shop near me" — highest intent, location-aware, maps-integrated
- "farm shops [county name]" — planning/research queries, high volume in rural counties
- "honesty box near me" — currently underserved; Farmmap has potential to own this
- "farm shops open [day]" — operational queries
- "buy local food [town/city]" — awareness queries
- "farm shop online delivery [area]" — V3-relevant future queries

**Traffic volume estimates:**
- "farm shop near me" — estimated 40,000–60,000 UK monthly searches [inference from
  Google Keyword Planner public-tier estimates; not verified; directional only]
- "farm shops [county]" — approximately 500–2,000/month per major county [inference]
- "honesty boxes" or "honesty box near me" — approximately 2,000–5,000 UK/Ireland
  combined [inference; this is a low-competition query Farmmap could potentially own]

**SEO strategy priorities for 100k visitor target:**
1. Schema.org LocalBusiness markup on all 953 listings from launch day — enables
   rich snippets in Google Maps and search results
2. Landing pages per county/region: "Best Farm Shops in [County]" — structured,
   SEO-optimised county guide pages targeting high-intent planning queries
3. Technical excellence: Core Web Vitals, mobile-first, fast tile loading — should
   outperform BigBarn on all technical metrics from day one
4. Backlinks from food and rural lifestyle media: Country Life, The Grocer, BBC
   Good Food, The Guardian's food section — target editorial features, not paid
   placements

**Timeline:** SEO compounding typically takes 6–12 months for a new domain targeting
competitive queries. With a modern technical stack and 953 structured listing pages,
Farmmap has a structural advantage over BigBarn. Realistic SEO contribution to the
100k target: 40,000–60,000 monthly sessions at Month 12 if content programme is
fully executed.

**CAC from SEO:** Near-zero marginal CAC per consumer — SEO is a fixed-cost
investment (editorial content, technical optimisation) with zero incremental cost
per session. This is the highest-leverage consumer acquisition channel.

---

#### Channel C2 — Social Media (Supporting)

**Primary platform:** Instagram — visual food content, rural lifestyle, foraging and
seasonal produce are organic to Instagram's content ecosystem. Farm shop photography
(misty mornings, baskets of veg, hand-painted signs) performs well on Instagram.

**Secondary platform:** TikTok — "hidden gem" food discoveries and "farm shop hauls"
are an active content genre with 10M+ combined views on #farmshop content [inference
from TikTok content audit; specific view counts are estimates]

**Approach:** Farmmap should build its own social channels but also enable and
amplify user-generated content — consumers posting about farm shops they found on
Farmmap. The "Find local. Support local." tagline is a social-native call to action.

**CAC from social:** Low-cost but requires consistent brand-building investment.
Not a direct traffic driver at launch scale; becomes a compounding asset over 12–24
months as the content archive grows.

#### Channel C3 — PR and Editorial Coverage (Launch Amplification)

**Target outlets:**
- Rural and countryside press: Country Life, Country Living, The Spectator countryside
  pages, This Is Money food section
- Regional press: local newspaper "local food" features are common and have high
  geographic targeting
- Food media: BBC Good Food, Guardian Food, Waitrose Food magazine — all feature
  "buy local" editorial regularly
- Agricultural trade press: Farmers Weekly, Farming UK — relevant for farm shop
  owner awareness (supply-side PR)

**Launch PR angle:**
"The UK's most complete map of farm shops and honesty boxes — finally, all on one
map." The honesty box angle is genuinely novel for national food press. "Britain's
hidden economy of honesty boxes mapped for the first time" is a plausible national
newspaper story.

**CAC from PR:** Zero direct CAC — editorial coverage is earned. One national
food press feature can deliver 5,000–20,000 sessions. Requires a press contact
and story pitch as part of the launch plan.

### Farm Shop Owner Channels

#### Channel S1 — FRA Partnership (Highest Leverage)

**Why this matters:**
The FRA has approximately 1,200 member businesses and direct communication channels
(newsletter, annual events, regional meetings). A single FRA endorsement email to
members is the equivalent of 1,200 warm introductions. The discovery-decision
document makes FRA partnership a named condition for V1 launch.

**Approach:** Co-existence positioning. Farmmap offers:
- To list FRA membership status as a badge on qualifying listings
- To act as a free additional discovery channel for FRA members
- To share aggregated anonymous consumer traffic data with FRA for their sector
  research
- A formal data-sharing agreement replacing the informal FRA data use at launch

**Expected outcome:** If FRA endorses Farmmap, Bronze conversion rate among FRA
members should be 2–3× the cold-outreach rate. FRA members are digitally engaged
relative to non-member farm shops (FRA membership self-selects for business
development interest).

**CAC via FRA channel:** If FRA newsletter drives 50 Bronze sign-ups, and the cost
of securing the FRA relationship is 10 founder hours + a reciprocal data sharing
arrangement, CAC per Bronze shop via this channel is approximately £0 monetary cost
(founder time is a sunk cost, not a cash outlay). This is by far the lowest CAC
channel and should be prioritised above all others.

---

#### Channel S2 — Direct Outreach / Regional Rep Visits (Highest Conversion)

**The saleability critique is explicit:** cold email and social media will not achieve
meaningful claim rates with the farm shop owner demographic (50–70, rural, owner-
operated). Personal visits from a regional rep convert at 20–30% — vastly better than
cold email (<5% open rate).

**Economics:**
- Rep visit cost: £80–£150 per visit (travel + time) [from saleability-critique.md]
- Conversion rate: 20–30% of visited shops sign up for free claim; 20–30% of those
  convert to Bronze within 12 months
- Net Bronze conversion per rep visit: 20–30% × 20–30% = 4–9% of all rep visits
- Cost per Bronze subscriber via rep visit: £80–£150 ÷ 4–9% = **£889–£3,750/subscriber**

This is dramatically higher than the saleability critique's headline CAC of £120–£250.
The discrepancy is because the £120–£250 figure assumes some passive claim rate on
top of the rep programme, reducing the average. The true direct CAC from rep visits
alone is much higher.

**Implication:** Rep visits should target the highest-value prospects: shops with
high Farmmap listing traffic (demonstrating consumer demand already exists), shops
in high-density areas (multiple shops per visit), and FRA members who have received
the FRA endorsement communication and are warm leads.

**CAC via direct outreach (targeted):** £120–£250 per Bronze subscriber when rep
visits are combined with traffic evidence and pre-warmed FRA referrals. Without
those conditions, CAC via rep alone approaches £500–£1,000.

---

#### Channel S3 — Regional Agricultural Shows (Relationship Building)

**UK agricultural shows with farm retail relevance:**
- Royal Agricultural Society of England (RASE) events
- County shows: Royal Bath & West, Royal Welsh, Royal Highland, Royal Ulster
- Farm Retail Association annual conference (primary farm shop owner event)

**Role:** Brand awareness and relationship building with farm shop owners in their
professional community. Not a direct Bronze conversion channel at scale. Farmmap
presence at 2–3 shows per year positions it as part of the farm retail community,
not an outside tech intruder.

**CAC via shows:** High (show stand cost £1,000–£5,000 per event, staff time,
materials) for direct conversions. Value is relationship and credibility investment
rather than direct acquisition. Priority: FRA Annual Conference above all others.

---

#### Channel S4 — Cold Email / Digital Outreach (Low Yield but Scalable)

**Reality check from saleability critique:** Sub-5% open rate, lower still click-
through and conversion. Cold email to farm shop owners is not a primary acquisition
channel.

**Where it works:**
- As a follow-up after consumer traffic has generated listing page views. If a farm
  shop's listing receives 200+ sessions/month, an automated outreach email ("Your
  listing was viewed 212 times in March — claim it free to update your photos and
  hours") has a very different conversion profile from a cold "join our directory"
  email.
- As part of a sequenced campaign: FRA newsletter mention → listing page view spike
  → personalised traffic email → rep call. This is a 3-stage warm funnel, not cold
  email.

**CAC via cold email alone:** Not worth calculating — conversion rates are too low
to make this the primary channel. Value only as part of a warmed funnel.

### Channel Summary: Realistic CAC per Farm Shop Owner

| Channel | CAC per Bronze Subscriber | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| FRA partnership referral | ~£0 monetary / ~£500 time equivalent | Highest leverage; requires relationship investment |
| Traffic-triggered personalised outreach | £20–£40 | Automatable; requires V1 traffic to trigger |
| Rep visit (targeted, warm leads) | £120–£250 | Saleability critique estimate; requires pre-warming |
| Rep visit (cold territory) | £500–£1,500 | Not economically viable at scale |
| Agricultural shows | £500–£2,000 | Relationship investment, not direct conversion |
| Cold email alone | £2,000+ | Not viable as standalone channel |

**Recommended CAC budget for Year 1 Bronze acquisition programme:**
- Priority 1: FRA relationship (invest in securing partnership; low monetary CAC)
- Priority 2: SEO-driven consumer traffic → traffic-triggered email funnel
- Priority 3: Rep visits in high-density areas with evidence of listing views
- Avoid: cold mass email campaigns, agricultural show stands in Year 1 (returns
  too low relative to brand maturity)
